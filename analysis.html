Sequences Practice Questions

1. Let \(a_n = \frac{n + (-1)^n}{n}\). Determine whether the sequence \(\{a_n\}\) converges, and if so, find its limit.

A) Converges to 0
B) Converges to 1/2
C) Converges to 1
D) Converges to 2
E) Diverges

Show/Hide Explanation

Rewrite \(a_n = \frac{n + (-1)^n}{n} = 1 + \frac{(-1)^n}{n}\). As \(n \to \infty\), \(\frac{(-1)^n}{n} \to 0\) since the numerator oscillates between \(-1\) and \(1\), and the denominator grows without bound. Thus, \(a_n \to 1\). By the **Limit Theorem for Sequences**, if the terms approach a finite value, the sequence converges.

Answer: C) Converges to 1

2. Consider the sequence defined by \(a_1 = 1\), \(a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + a_n}\) for \(n \geq 1\). Does the sequence converge, and if so, to what value?

A) Converges to 1
B) Converges to 2
C) Converges to \(\sqrt{2}\)
D) Converges to 3
E) Diverges

Show/Hide Explanation

First, show the sequence is bounded: \(a_1 = 1 < 2\), and if \(a_n < 2\), then \(a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + a_n} < \sqrt{2 + 2} = 2\). Thus, \(a_n < 2\). Next, show it is increasing: \(a_{n+1}^2 = 2 + a_n > a_n^2\) since \(a_n < 2\). By the **Monotone Convergence Theorem**, a bounded, increasing sequence converges. Assume the limit is \(L\): then \(L = \sqrt{2 + L}\). Solving \(L^2 = 2 + L\), we get \(L^2 - L - 2 = 0\), so \(L = 2\) or \(L = -1\). Since \(a_n > 0\), the limit is 2.

Answer: B) Converges to 2

3. Let \(a_n = n \sin\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\). Determine whether the sequence \(\{a_n\}\) converges, and if so, find its limit.

A) Converges to 0
B) Converges to 1
C) Converges to \(\pi\)
D) Converges to \(\sin(1)\)
E) Diverges

Show/Hide Explanation

Rewrite \(a_n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)}{\frac{1}{n}}\). As \(n \to \infty\), \(\frac{1}{n} \to 0\), so use the fact that \(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1\). Thus, \(a_n \to 1\). However, the question is tricky: one might expect divergence due to \(n\), but the **Squeeze Theorem** confirms convergence since \(\sin\left(\frac{1}{n}\right) \approx \frac{1}{n}\) for small \(\frac{1}{n}\). The correct answer is not listed, indicating a possible error in the options; however, based on standard real analysis, it converges to 1.

Answer: B) Converges to 1 (Note: Corrected option label to match intent)

Numerical Series Practice Questions

1. Determine the convergence of the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n} + (-1)^n}\).

A) Converges absolutely
B) Converges conditionally
C) Diverges
D) Converges to 0
E) Converges to 1

Show/Hide Explanation

The series is alternating. Apply the **Alternating Series Test**: Let \(a_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + (-1)^n}\). As \(n \to \infty\), \(a_n \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\), which is decreasing and approaches 0. Thus, the series converges. For absolute convergence, consider \(\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + (-1)^n} \geq \sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + 1}\). Since \(\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\) diverges (p-series, \(p = 1/2\)), the series does not converge absolutely. By the **Absolute Convergence Test**, it converges conditionally.

Answer: B) Converges conditionally

2. Consider the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\sin(n)}{n^2}\). Does it converge?

A) Diverges
B) Converges conditionally
C) Converges absolutely
D) Converges to 0
E) Converges to \(\sin(1)\)

Show/Hide Explanation

Test for absolute convergence: \(\sum \left| \frac{\sin(n)}{n^2} \right| \leq \sum \frac{1}{n^2}\), since \(|\sin(n)| \leq 1\). The series \(\sum \frac{1}{n^2}\) converges (p-series, \(p = 2\)). By the **Comparison Test**, \(\sum \frac{|\sin(n)|}{n^2}\) converges, so the series converges absolutely. By the **Absolute Convergence Theorem**, absolute convergence implies convergence.

Answer: C) Converges absolutely

3. Let \(\sum a_n\) be a series such that \(a_n > 0\) and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{a_n} = 2\). What can be said about its convergence?

A) Diverges
B) Converges absolutely
C) Converges conditionally
D) Converges to 1
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

By the **Root Test**, if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{a_n} = 2 > 1\), the series \(\sum a_n\) diverges. This is a tricky question because one might assume convergence due to positive terms, but the root test definitively determines divergence.

Answer: A) Diverges

Limits Practice Questions

1. Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln(x)\).

A) \(\infty\)
B) 0
C) -1
D) 1
E) Does not exist

Show/Hide Explanation

The limit is of the form \(0 \cdot (-\infty)\). Rewrite \(x \ln(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\frac{1}{x}}\), which is \(\frac{-\infty}{\infty}\). Apply **L'Hôpital's Rule**: differentiate numerator (\(\frac{1}{x}\)) and denominator (\(-\frac{1}{x^2}\)). The limit becomes \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{-\frac{1}{x^2}} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) = 0\). Thus, the limit is 0.

Answer: B) 0

2. Let \(f(x) = \lfloor x \rfloor \sin(\pi x)\). Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x)\).

A) 0
B) 1
C) -1
D) 2
E) Does not exist

Show/Hide Explanation

For \(x \to 2^-\), \(1 < x < 2\), so \(\lfloor x \rfloor = 1\). Thus, \(f(x) = \sin(\pi x)\). Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} \sin(\pi x)\). As \(x \to 2^-\), \(\pi x \to 2\pi^-\), and \(\sin(\pi x) \to \sin(2\pi) = 0\). However, the question is tricky: the function oscillates, but the left-hand limit exists. Re-evaluate: as \(x \to 2^-\), \(\lfloor x \rfloor = 1\), but we need the behavior of \(\sin(\pi x)\). This suggests a possible error; let’s check the right limit for comparison. For \(x \to 2^+\), \(\lfloor x \rfloor = 2\), so the limit differs, but the question specifies \(2^-\). Thus, the answer is 0 (corrected option).

Answer: A) 0 (Note: Corrected option label)

3. Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x\).

A) \(e\)
B) 1
C) 0
D) \(\infty\)
E) Does not exist

Show/Hide Explanation

This is a classic limit. Rewrite \(\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x\). As \(x \to \infty\), use the fact that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e\), a standard result from real analysis. Alternatively, take the natural log: \(\ln\left(\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x\right) = x \ln\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right) \approx x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = 1\), but more precisely, \(\ln\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right) \approx \frac{1}{x}\), so the limit is \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = 1\), and thus the original limit is \(e^1 = e\). By the **Continuity of the Exponential Function**, the limit is \(e\).

Answer: A) \(e\)

Continuity Practice Questions

1. Define \(f(x) = \begin{cases} x \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0 \end{cases}\). Is \(f\) continuous at \(x = 0\)?

A) Not continuous
B) Continuous
C) Continuous but not differentiable
D) Differentiable
E) Uniformly continuous

Show/Hide Explanation

For continuity at \(x = 0\), check \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x) = f(0) = 0\). Since \(|\sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)| \leq 1\), we have \(|x \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)| \leq |x|\). By the **Squeeze Theorem**, as \(x \to 0\), \(|x| \to 0\), so \(\lim_{x \to 0} x \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0\). Thus, \(f\) is continuous at \(x = 0\). The question is tricky because one might suspect discontinuity due to oscillation, but the squeeze theorem ensures continuity.

Answer: B) Continuous

2. Let \(f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x \text{ is rational} \\ 0 & x \text{ is irrational} \end{cases}\). Is \(f\) continuous anywhere?

A) Nowhere continuous
B) Continuous at \(x = 0\)
C) Continuous at rational points
D) Continuous at irrational points
E) Everywhere continuous

Show/Hide Explanation

For continuity at \(x = c\), \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\). Since rationals and irrationals are dense in \(\mathbb{R}\), for any \(c\), a sequence of rationals \(x_n \to c\) gives \(f(x_n) = 1\), and a sequence of irrationals \(y_n \to c\) gives \(f(y_n) = 0\). Thus, the limit does not exist unless \(f(c) = 1\) and \(f(c) = 0\), which is impossible. By the **Sequential Criterion for Continuity**, \(f\) is nowhere continuous.

Answer: A) Nowhere continuous

3. Suppose \(f: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}\) is continuous and satisfies \(f(x) = f(x^2)\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\). What is \(f(0)\)?

A) 1
B) 0
C) \(f(1)\)
D) \(f(0^2)\)
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

Since \(f(x) = f(x^2)\), evaluate at \(x = 0\): \(f(0) = f(0^2) = f(0)\), which is true but uninformative. Now, evaluate at \(x = 1\): \(f(1) = f(1^2) = f(1)\). Consider the sequence \(x_n = 0^{2^n} = 0\). By continuity and the **Fixed Point Property** of continuous functions on \([0,1]\), \(f(x_n) = f(0)\), and since \(x_n \to 0\), \(\lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) = f(0)\). Now try \(x = \sqrt{1} = 1\): \(f(1) = f(1)\). The key is to test points: if \(x = \sqrt{x}\), then \(x = 0\) or \(x = 1\). Thus, \(f(0) = f(0^2) = f(0)\), and \(f(1) = f(1^2) = f(1)\). The tricky part is realizing \(f(0) = f(1)\) due to the functional equation and continuity on a closed interval. Thus, \(f(0) = f(1)\).

Answer: C) \(f(1)\)

Differentiation Practice Questions

1. Let \(f(x) = |x| \sin(x)\). Is \(f\) differentiable at \(x = 0\)?

A) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 0\)
B) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 1\)
C) Yes, and \(f'(0) = -1\)
D) No
E) Yes, but \(f'(0)\) is undefined

Show/Hide Explanation

Check differentiability at \(x = 0\): compute \(f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h) - f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h| \sin(h)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \text{sgn}(h) \sin(h)\). For \(h > 0\), this is \(\sin(h) \to 0\); for \(h < 0\), this is \(-\sin(h) \to 0\). Since both one-sided limits equal 0, \(f'(0) = 0\). By the **Definition of the Derivative**, \(f\) is differentiable at \(x = 0\).

Answer: A) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 0\)

2. Suppose \(f\) is differentiable on \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(|f'(x)| \leq 1\) for all \(x\). If \(f(0) = 0\), what is the largest possible value of \(f(2)\)?

A) 1
B) 2
C) 0
D) 4
E) \(\infty\)

Show/Hide Explanation

By the **Mean Value Theorem**, for some \(c \in (0,2)\), \(f(2) - f(0) = f'(c) \cdot (2 - 0)\). Since \(|f'(c)| \leq 1\), we have \(|f(2)| \leq 2\). The largest possible value occurs when \(f'(c) = 1\), so \(f(2) = 2\). For example, \(f(x) = x\) satisfies \(f'(x) = 1\) and \(f(2) = 2\). Thus, the maximum is 2.

Answer: B) 2

3. Let \(f(x) = x^2 \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\) for \(x \neq 0\), and \(f(0) = 0\). Is \(f\) differentiable at \(x = 0\)?

A) No
B) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 1\)
C) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 0\)
D) Yes, and \(f'(0) = \sin(1)\)
E) Yes, but \(f'(0)\) is undefined

Show/Hide Explanation

Compute \(f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h) - f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^2 \sin\left(\frac{1}{h}\right)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h \sin\left(\frac{1}{h}\right)\). Since \(|\sin\left(\frac{1}{h}\right)| \leq 1\), \(|h \sin\left(\frac{1}{h}\right)| \leq |h|\). By the **Squeeze Theorem**, as \(h \to 0\), \(|h| \to 0\), so the limit is 0. Thus, \(f\) is differentiable at \(x = 0\), and \(f'(0) = 0\). The oscillation of \(\sin\left(\frac{1}{h}\right)\) makes this tricky, but the \(h^2\) term ensures differentiability.

Answer: C) Yes, and \(f'(0) = 0\)

Integration Practice Questions

1. Evaluate \(\int_0^1 \frac{\ln(1 + x)}{x} \, dx\).

A) 1
B) \(\frac{\pi^2}{12}\)
C) \(\frac{\pi^2}{6}\)
D) \(\ln(2)\)
E) Diverges

Show/Hide Explanation

Use the series expansion \(\ln(1 + x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} \frac{x^n}{n}\) for \(|x| < 1\). Then, \(\frac{\ln(1 + x)}{x} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} \frac{x^{n-1}}{n}\). Integrate term-by-term: \(\int_0^1 \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} \frac{x^{n-1}}{n} \, dx = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} \int_0^1 x^{n-1} \, dx = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} \cdot \frac{1}{n} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n^2}\). This is the Dirichlet eta function: \(\eta(2) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{12}\). By the **Term-by-Term Integration Theorem** for uniformly convergent series, the integral converges to \(\frac{\pi^2}{12}\).

Answer: B) \(\frac{\pi^2}{12}\)

2. Consider the improper integral \(\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(x)}{x} \, dx\). Does it converge?

A) Diverges
B) Converges to 0
C) Converges to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\)
D) Converges to 1
E) Converges to \(\pi\)

Show/Hide Explanation

This is the Dirichlet integral. The integrand \(\frac{\sin(x)}{x}\) is continuous for \(x \neq 0\), and at \(x = 0\), \(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1\), so define \(f(0) = 1\). The integral \(\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(x)}{x} \, dx\) is improper. Split it: \(\int_0^1 + \int_1^\infty\). The first part converges since \(\frac{\sin(x)}{x}\) is bounded. For the second, use **Dirichlet's Test for Integrals**: \(\sin(x)\) is bounded, and \(\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x} \, dx\) diverges slowly, but the oscillatory nature of \(\sin(x)\) ensures convergence to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\), a standard result in real analysis.

Answer: C) Converges to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\)

3. Let \(f_n(x) = n x (1 - x^2)^n\) on \([0,1]\). Evaluate \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f_n(x) \, dx\).

A) \(\frac{1}{2}\)
B) 0
C) 1
D) \(\infty\)
E) Does not exist

Show/Hide Explanation

Compute \(\int_0^1 n x (1 - x^2)^n \, dx\). Substitute \(u = 1 - x^2\), so \(du = -2x \, dx\), \(x \, dx = -\frac{du}{2}\), and limits \(x = 0 \to u = 1\), \(x = 1 \to u = 0\). The integral becomes \(\int_1^0 n \left(-\frac{du}{2}\right) u^n = \frac{n}{2} \int_0^1 u^n \, du = \frac{n}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{n+1} = \frac{n}{2(n+1)}\). As \(n \to \infty\), \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{2(n+1)} = \frac{1}{2}\). By the **Limit of Integrals Theorem**, the limit exists and equals \(\frac{1}{2}\).

Answer: A) \(\frac{1}{2}\)

Series of Functions Practice Questions

1. Consider the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n^2}\) on \([-1,1]\). Determine where it converges uniformly.

A) On \([-1,1]\)
B) On \([-1,1)\)
C) On \((-1,1]\)
D) On \((-1,1)\)
E) Nowhere

Show/Hide Explanation

Use the **Weierstrass M-test**: For \(|x| \leq 1\), \(\left| \frac{x^n}{n^2} \right| \leq \frac{1}{n^2}\). Since \(\sum \frac{1}{n^2} < \infty\), the series converges uniformly on \([-1,1]\). However, at \(x = 1\), \(\sum \frac{1}{n^2}\) converges, but at \(x = -1\), \(\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}\) converges conditionally. The M-test applies to absolute convergence, so uniform convergence holds on \([-1,1]\), but the question is tricky: the boundary at \(x = 1\) is included, so \([-1,1)\) is incorrect. Correct answer is \([-1,1]\).

Answer: A) On \([-1,1]\) (Note: Corrected option label)

2. Let \(f_n(x) = \frac{n x}{1 + n^2 x^2}\) on \(\mathbb{R}\). What is the pointwise limit of \(f_n(x)\)?

A) \(f(x) = x\)
B) \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\)
C) \(f(x) = 0\)
D) \(f(x) = 1\)
E) Does not exist

Show/Hide Explanation

For fixed \(x\), compute \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n x}{1 + n^2 x^2}\). Rewrite as \(\frac{\frac{x}{n}}{\frac{1}{n^2} + x^2}\). As \(n \to \infty\), the numerator \(\frac{x}{n} \to 0\), and the denominator \(\frac{1}{n^2} + x^2 \to x^2\) (if \(x \neq 0\)). Thus, the limit is 0. At \(x = 0\), \(f_n(0) = 0\), so the limit is 0. By the **Pointwise Convergence Theorem**, the limit function is \(f(x) = 0\).

Answer: C) \(f(x) = 0\)

3. Consider the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^n\) on \((-1,1)\). Is the sum differentiable on \((-1,1)\)?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Only at \(x = 0\)
D) Only on \((-1/2,1/2)\)
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

The series is \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^n\). Its sum is \(f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^n = x \sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^{n-1} = x \cdot \frac{1}{(1-x)^2} = \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}\) for \(|x| < 1\). The derivative series is \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^2 x^{n-1}\). Check uniform convergence of the derivative series on \([-r,r]\), \(0 < r < 1\), using the **Weierstrass M-test**: \(\left| n^2 x^{n-1} \right| \leq n^2 r^{n-1}\), and \(\sum n^2 r^{n-1} < \infty\). Thus, the derivative series converges uniformly on compact subsets of \((-1,1)\). By the **Term-by-Term Differentiation Theorem**, the series is differentiable on \((-1,1)\).

Answer: A) Yes
