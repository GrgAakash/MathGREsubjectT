1. Evaluate \( \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\cos(3x)-1}{x^2} \)

Source: (1),Practice 1, Q1

\( \frac{9}{2} \)
\( \frac{3}{2} \)
\( -\frac{2}{3} \)
\( -\frac{3}{2} \)
\( -\frac{9}{2} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Use the limit identity: \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x - 1}{x^2} = -\frac{1}{2} \). Let \( u = 3x \), then the limit becomes \( 9 \cdot (-\frac{1}{2}) = -\frac{9}{2} \).

Answer: (E) \( -\frac{9}{2} \)

2. Let \( g(x) = e^{2x + 1} \). Evaluate \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g(g(x)) - g(e)}{x} \)

Source: (1),Practice 1, Q20

\( 2e \)
\( 4e \)
\( e \)
\( 2e^{2e+1} \)
\( 4e^{2e+2} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Solution:

  1. First compute \( g(g(x)) \): \[ g(g(x)) = e^{2e^{2x+1} + 1} \]
  2. At \( x = 0 \): \[ g(e) = e^{2e + 1} \]
  3. The limit is a 0/0 form, so apply L'Hôpital's Rule: \[ \frac{d}{dx}[g(g(x))] = g'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \] \[ g'(x) = 2e^{2x+1} \]
  4. Evaluate at \( x = 0 \): \[ g'(0) = 2e \] \[ g'(e) = 2e^{2e+1} \] \[ \text{Limit} = g'(e) \cdot g'(0) = 4e^{2e+2} \]

Answer: (E) \( 4e^{2e+2} \)

4. Let \( \varphi(x) = \int_0^x \varphi(t)\,dt + \sin x \). Then \( \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \left(2\varphi(x) - e^x \right) \) is

Source: (2), Page 39

1
0
\( \cos 1 \)
\( \sin 1 \)
\( \frac{1}{2} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Differentiating both sides of \( \varphi(x) = \int_0^x \varphi(t)\,dt + \sin x \) gives \( \varphi'(x) = \varphi(x) + \cos x \).

Solving the differential equation yields \( \varphi(x) = \frac{\sin x - \cos x}{2} + \frac{e^x}{2} \).

Then \( 2\varphi(x) - e^x = \sin x - \cos x \), and as \( x \to \pi/2 \), \( \sin x \to 1 \), \( \cos x \to 0 \).

Answer: (A) 1

5. Let \( y = x^{x^{x^{\cdots}}} \). Then \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) equals

Source: (2), Page 39

\( y \ln x \)
\( y \ln y \)
\( y \ln y \ln x \)
\( \frac{y \ln y}{x} \)
\( \frac{y}{x} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Let \( y = x^y \). Taking natural logs: \( \ln y = y \ln x \). Differentiating: \( \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dx} \ln x + \frac{y}{x} \).

Solving gives \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y \ln y}{x} \).

Answer: (D) \( \frac{y \ln y}{x} \)

8. Evaluate \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\tan x - \sin x}{x^3} \)

Source: (2), Page 39

\( \frac{1}{3} \)
\( \frac{2}{3} \)
\( \frac{3}{2} \)
\( \frac{5}{6} \)
1

Show/Hide Explanation

Use Taylor expansions: \( \tan x = x + \frac{x^3}{3} + \dots \), \( \sin x = x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \dots \).

So, numerator \( \approx \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{x^3}{6} = \frac{x^3}{2} \), and the limit is \( \frac{1}{2} \).

Answer: (E) 1

10. Evaluate \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^{\tan x} - \tan x - 1}{x^2} \)

Source: (2), Page 39

\( \frac{1}{2} \)
1
\( \frac{3}{2} \)
2
\( \frac{5}{2} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Use Taylor expansions: \( \tan x = x + \frac{x^3}{3} + \dots \), so \( e^{\tan x} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{6} + \dots \).

Subtracting \( \tan x + 1 \), the numerator becomes \( \frac{x^2}{2} + \dots \). So the limit is \( \frac{1}{2} \).

Answer: (A) \( \frac{1}{2} \)
