1. Let \( X = \{a, b, c\} \) with topology \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \). Is the set \( \{b, c\} \) open in \( X \)?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Open and closed
D) Neither open nor closed
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

A set is open if it belongs to the topology \( \tau \). The topology is \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \). Since \( \{b, c\} \notin \tau \), it is not open. By the definition of a topology, only sets in \( \tau \) are open.

Answer: B) No

2. In the same space \( X = \{a, b, c\} \), \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \), what is the closure of \( \{b\} \)?

A) \( \{b\} \)
B) \( \{b, c\} \)
C) \( \{a, b\} \)
D) \( X \)
E) \( \emptyset \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The closure of \( \{b\} \) is the smallest closed set containing \( \{b\} \). Closed sets are complements of open sets: complements of \( \emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X \) are \( X, \{b, c\}, \{c\}, \emptyset \). The smallest closed set containing \( \{b\} \) is \( \{b, c\} \), since \( b \in \{b, c\} \) and it is closed.

Answer: B) \( \{b, c\} \)

3. In \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology, which of the following is both open and closed?

A) \( (0, 1) \)
B) \( [0, 1] \)
C) \( \mathbb{R} \)
D) \( \{0\} \)
E) \( (-\infty, 0] \)

Show/Hide Explanation

In any topological space, the entire space \( X \) and the empty set \( \emptyset \) are both open and closed. In \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology, \( (0, 1) \) is open but not closed, \( [0, 1] \) is closed but not open, \( \{0\} \) is closed but not open, and \( (-\infty, 0] \) is closed but not open. Only \( \mathbb{R} \) is both open and closed by definition.

Answer: C) \( \mathbb{R} \)

4. In the discrete topology on a set \( X \), which sets are open?

A) Only \( \emptyset \)
B) Only \( X \)
C) Only singletons
D) All subsets of \( X \)
E) Only finite subsets

Show/Hide Explanation

In the discrete topology, every subset of \( X \) is open, as the topology includes all possible subsets. This is a defining property of the discrete topology, making every set both open and closed.

Answer: D) All subsets of \( X \)

5. Is the set \( [0, \infty) \) compact in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Compact but not connected
D) Connected but not compact
E) Neither compact nor connected

Show/Hide Explanation

In \( \mathbb{R} \), a set is compact if it is closed and bounded (Heine-Borel Theorem). The set \( [0, \infty) \) is closed but unbounded. Thus, it is not compact. Since it is an interval, it is connected.

Answer: D) Connected but not compact

6. Let \( X = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} \cup \{0\} \) in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology. Is \( X \) compact?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Compact but not closed
D) Closed but not compact
E) Neither compact nor closed

Show/Hide Explanation

By the Heine-Borel Theorem, \( X \) is compact if closed and bounded. \( X = \{1, 1/2, 1/3, \ldots, 0\} \) is bounded (in \([0, 1]\)) and closed (includes its limit point 0). Thus, \( X \) is compact.

Answer: A) Yes

7. Is the set \( (0, 1) \cup (1, 2) \) connected in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Connected but not path-connected
D) Path-connected but not connected
E) Neither connected nor compact

Show/Hide Explanation

A set is connected if it cannot be written as the union of two non-empty, disjoint open sets. The set \( (0, 1) \cup (1, 2) \) is the union of two open sets, \( (0, 1) \) and \( (1, 2) \), which are non-empty and disjoint. Therefore, \( (0, 1) \cup (1, 2) \) is not connected.

Answer: B) No
