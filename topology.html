General Topology Practice Questions

1. Let \( X = \{a, b, c\} \) with topology \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \). Is the set \( \{b, c\} \) open in \( X \)?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Open and closed
D) Neither open nor closed
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

A set is open if it belongs to the topology \( \tau \). The topology is \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \). Since \( \{b, c\} \notin \tau \), it is not open. By the definition of a topology, only sets in \( \tau \) are open.

Answer: B) No

2. In the same space \( X = \{a, b, c\} \), \( \tau = \{\emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X\} \), what is the closure of \( \{b\} \)?

A) \( \{b\} \)
B) \( \{b, c\} \)
C) \( \{a, b\} \)
D) \( X \)
E) \( \emptyset \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The closure of \( \{b\} \) is the smallest closed set containing \( \{b\} \). Closed sets are complements of open sets: complements of \( \emptyset, \{a\}, \{a, b\}, X \) are \( X, \{b, c\}, \{c\}, \emptyset \). The smallest closed set containing \( \{b\} \) is \( \{b, c\} \), since \( b \in \{b, c\} \) and it is closed.

Answer: B) \( \{b, c\} \)

3. In \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology, which of the following is both open and closed?

A) \( (0, 1) \)
B) \( [0, 1] \)
C) \( \mathbb{R} \)
D) \( \{0\} \)
E) \( (-\infty, 0] \)

Show/Hide Explanation

In any topological space, the entire space \( X \) and the empty set \( \emptyset \) are both open and closed. In \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology, \( (0, 1) \) is open but not closed, \( [0, 1] \) is closed but not open, \( \{0\} \) is closed but not open, and \( (-\infty, 0] \) is closed but not open. Only \( \mathbb{R} \) is both open and closed by definition.

Answer: C) \( \mathbb{R} \)

4. In the discrete topology on a set \( X \), which sets are open?

A) Only \( \emptyset \)
B) Only \( X \)
C) Only singletons
D) All subsets of \( X \)
E) Only finite subsets

Show/Hide Explanation

In the discrete topology, every subset of \( X \) is open, as the topology includes all possible subsets. This is a defining property of the discrete topology, making every set both open and closed.

Answer: D) All subsets of \( X \)

5. Is the set \( [0, \infty) \) compact in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Compact but not connected
D) Connected but not compact
E) Neither compact nor connected

Show/Hide Explanation

In \( \mathbb{R} \), a set is compact if it is closed and bounded (Heine-Borel Theorem). The set \( [0, \infty) \) is closed but unbounded. Thus, it is not compact. Since it is an interval, it is connected.

Answer: D) Connected but not compact

6. Let \( X = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} \cup \{0\} \) in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology. Is \( X \) compact?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Compact but not closed
D) Closed but not compact
E) Neither compact nor closed

Show/Hide Explanation

By the Heine-Borel Theorem, \( X \) is compact if closed and bounded. \( X = \{1, 1/2, 1/3, \ldots, 0\} \) is bounded (in \([0, 1]\)) and closed (includes its limit point 0). Thus, \( X \) is compact.

Answer: A) Yes

7. Is the set \( (0, 1) \cup (1, 2) \) connected in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Connected but not path-connected
D) Path-connected but not connected
E) Neither connected nor compact

Show/Hide Explanation

A set is connected if it cannot be written as the union of two non-empty, disjoint open sets. The set \( (0, 1) \cup (1, 2) \) is the union of \( (0, 1) \) and \( (1, 2) \), both open and disjoint, so it is not connected.

Answer: B) No

8. Is \( \mathbb{Q} \) (the rationals) connected in \( \mathbb{R} \) with the standard topology?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Connected but not compact
D) Compact but not connected
E) Path-connected

Show/Hide Explanation

Take open sets \( (-\infty, \sqrt{2}) \cap \mathbb{Q} \) and \( (\sqrt{2}, \infty) \cap \mathbb{Q} \). These are non-empty, disjoint, and cover \( \mathbb{Q} \), since \( \sqrt{2} \) is irrational. Thus, \( \mathbb{Q} \) is not connected.

Answer: B) No

9. Let \( f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \) be continuous, where \( \mathbb{R} \) has the standard topology. If \( f(x) = 0 \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{Q} \), what is \( f(x) \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \)?

A) \( f(x) = x \)
B) \( f(x) = 0 \)
C) \( f(x) = |x| \)
D) \( f(x) = \sin(\pi x) \)
E) \( f(x) = 1 \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Since \( f \) is continuous and \( \mathbb{Q} \) is dense in \( \mathbb{R} \), for any \( x \in \mathbb{R} \), there exists a sequence of \( q_n \in \mathbb{Q} \) such that \( q_n \to x \). Because \( f \) is continuous, \( \lim_{n \to \infty} f(q_n) = f(x) \). Since \( q_n \in \mathbb{Q} \), \( f(q_n) = 0 \) for all \( n \). Therefore, \( f(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} 0 = 0 \).

Answer: B) \( f(x) = 0 \)

10. Let \( X \) be a topological space and \( A \subseteq X \) be a subset. If \( X \) is Hausdorff, is \( A \) Hausdorff?

A) Yes
B) No
C) Only if A is closed
D) Only if A is open
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

A topological space \( X \) is Hausdorff if for any two distinct points \( x, y \in X \), there exist disjoint open neighborhoods \( U \) of \( x \) and \( V \) of \( y \). If \( X \) is Hausdorff, then for any two distinct points \( a, b \in A \), there exist disjoint open neighborhoods \( U \) of \( a \) and \( V \) of \( b \) in \( X \). Since \( A \subseteq X \), \( U \cap A \) and \( V \cap A \) are open subsets of \( A \) (in the relative topology) containing \( a \) and \( b \) respectively, and they are disjoint. Thus, \( A \) is Hausdorff.

Answer: A) Yes

Algebraic Topology Practice Questions

1. What is the fundamental group \( \pi_1(S^1) \) of the circle \( S^1 \)?

A) \( \{e\} \) (trivial group)
B) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The fundamental group of the circle \( S^1 \) is isomorphic to the group of integers, \( \mathbb{Z} \). This can be shown using the winding number of a loop on the circle.

Answer: C) \( \mathbb{Z} \)

2. What is the first homology group \( H_1(S^2) \) of the sphere \( S^2 \)?

A) \( 0 \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The integer homology groups of a sphere \( S^n \) are given by \( H_k(S^n) = \mathbb{Z} \) if \( k = 0 \) or \( k = n \), and \( H_k(S^n) = 0 \) otherwise. For \( S^2 \), \( H_1(S^2) = 0 \).

Answer: A) \( 0 \)

3. What is the fundamental group \( \pi_1(T^2) \) of the torus \( T^2 \)?

A) \( \{e\} \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The torus \( T^2 \) can be viewed as the product of two circles, \( S^1 \times S^1 \). The fundamental group of a product of two spaces is the direct product of their fundamental groups: \( \pi_1(S^1 \times S^1) = \pi_1(S^1) \oplus \pi_1(S^1) \). Since \( \pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z} \), \( \pi_1(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \).

Answer: D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)

4. What is the first homology group \( H_1(T^2) \) of the torus \( T^2 \)?

A) \( 0 \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The homology groups of a product of spaces are the direct sum of the homology groups: \( H_k(S^1 \times S^1) = H_k(S^1) \oplus H_k(S^1) \). For \( k=1 \), \( H_1(T^2) = H_1(S^1) \oplus H_1(S^1) \). Since \( H_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z} \), \( H_1(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \).

Answer: D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)

5. What is the Euler characteristic \( \chi(T^2) \) of the torus \( T^2 \)?

A) 0
B) 1
C) 2
D) -1
E) -2

Show/Hide Explanation

The Euler characteristic is defined as \( \chi(X) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (-1)^k \dim H_k(X) \). For the torus \( T^2 \), \( H_0(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \), \( H_1(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \), \( H_2(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \), and \( H_k(T^2) = 0 \) for \( k \ge 3 \). Thus, \( \chi(T^2) = \dim H_0(T^2) - \dim H_1(T^2) + \dim H_2(T^2) = 1 - 2 + 1 = 0 \).

Answer: A) 0

6. What is the Euler characteristic \( \chi(S^2) \) of the sphere \( S^2 \)?

A) 0
B) 1
C) 2
D) -1
E) -2

Show/Hide Explanation

For \( S^2 \), \( H_0(S^2) = \mathbb{Z} \), \( H_1(S^2) = 0 \), \( H_2(S^2) = \mathbb{Z} \), and \( H_k(S^2) = 0 \) for \( k \ge 3 \). Thus, \( \chi(S^2) = \dim H_0(S^2) - \dim H_1(S^2) + \dim H_2(S^2) = 1 - 0 + 1 = 2 \).

Answer: C) 2

7. What is the fundamental group \( \pi_1(\mathbb{R}^2) \) of the Euclidean plane \( \mathbb{R}^2 \)?

A) \( \{e\} \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The Euclidean plane \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) is simply connected, meaning that any loop can be continuously shrunk to a point. Therefore, its fundamental group is the trivial group \( \{e\} \).

Answer: A) \( \{e\} \)

8. What is the first homology group \( H_1(\mathbb{R}^2) \) of the Euclidean plane \( \mathbb{R}^2 \)?

A) \( 0 \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

Since \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) is simply connected, all its homology groups are zero. Therefore, \( H_1(\mathbb{R}^2) = 0 \).

Answer: A) \( 0 \)

9. What is the fundamental group \( \pi_1(RP^2) \) of the projective plane \( RP^2 \)?

A) \( \{e\} \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The fundamental group of the real projective plane \( RP^2 \) is isomorphic to the cyclic group of order 2, \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \). This can be shown by considering loops around the "point at infinity".

Answer: C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)

10. What is the first homology group \( H_1(RP^2) \) of the projective plane \( RP^2 \)?

A) \( 0 \)
B) \( \mathbb{Z} \)
C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
D) \( \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} \)
E) \( \mathbb{Q} \)

Show/Hide Explanation

The homology groups of the real projective plane \( RP^2 \) are given by \( H_k(RP^2) = \mathbb{Z} \) if \( k = 0 \), \( H_k(RP^2) = \mathbb{Z}_2 \) if \( k \) is even and \( k > 0 \), and \( H_k(RP^2) = 0 \) if \( k \) is odd. For \( RP^2 \), \( H_1(RP^2) = \mathbb{Z}_2 \).

Answer: C) \( \mathbb{Z}_2 \)
