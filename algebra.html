Group Theory Practice Questions

1. Let \(G\) be a group of order \(pq\), where \(p\) and \(q\) are distinct primes with \(p < q\). If \(q \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p}\), what can be said about \(G\)?

A) \(G\) is non-abelian
B) \(G\) has a normal subgroup of order \(p\)
C) \(G\) is cyclic
D) \(G\) has no subgroups of order \(q\)
E) \(G\) is isomorphic to \(S_p\)

Show/Hide Explanation

By **Sylowâ€™s Theorems**, for a group \(G\) of order \(pq\) (\(p < q\)), the number of Sylow \(q\)-subgroups, \(n_q\), divides \(p\) and satisfies \(n_q \equiv 1 \pmod{q}\). Since \(p < q\), the divisors of \(p\) are \(1\) and \(p\), and \(p \not\equiv 1 \pmod{q}\) (as \(q \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p}\)). Thus, \(n_q = 1\). Hence, there is exactly one Sylow \(q\)-subgroup, which is normal. By a standard result in group theory, if \(|G| = pq\) and the Sylow \(q\)-subgroup is normal, \(G\) is cyclic (isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}_{pq}\)).

Answer: C) \(G\) is cyclic

2. Let \(G\) be a finite group with a normal subgroup \(H\) such that \(|H| = 4\) and \(|G/H| = 3\). If \(G\) has no elements of order 12, what is the structure of \(G\)?

A) \(G \cong \mathbb{Z}_{12}\)
B) \(G \cong A_4\)
C) \(G \cong S_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_2\)
D) \(G \cong \mathbb{Z}_4 \times \mathbb{Z}_3\)
E) Cannot determine

Show/Hide Explanation

The order of \(G\) is \(|G| = |H| \cdot |G/H| = 4 \cdot 3 = 12\). By the **Fundamental Theorem of Finite Abelian Groups**, possible groups of order 12 are \(\mathbb{Z}_{12}\), \(\mathbb{Z}_6 \times \mathbb{Z}_2\), or non-abelian groups like \(A_4\) or \(D_6\). Since \(G\) has no elements of order 12, it cannot be \(\mathbb{Z}_{12}\) or \(\mathbb{Z}_6 \times \mathbb{Z}_2\) (both cyclic groups have an element of order 12). Consider \(A_4\), the alternating group on 4 elements, which has order 12 and a normal subgroup \(H \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2\) (the Klein four-group) with \(|H| = 4\). Then, \(A_4/H \cong \mathbb{Z}_3\), which has order 3. The elements of \(A_4\) (3-cycles, double transpositions) have orders 3 or 2, so no element has order 12. Thus, \(G \cong A_4\).

Answer: B) \(G \cong A_4\)

3. In the symmetric group \(S_5\), how many elements commute with the 5-cycle \((1 \ 2 \ 3 \ 4 \ 5)\)?

A) 5
B) 10
C) 20
D) 60
E) 120

Show/Hide Explanation

The number of elements commuting with a 5-cycle \(\sigma = (1 \ 2 \ 3 \ 4 \ 5)\) in \(S_5\) is the size of its centralizer, \(C_{S_5}(\sigma) = \{ g \in S_5 \mid g\sigma = \sigma g \}\). In a symmetric group, the centralizer of a cycle is related to its conjugacy class. A 5-cycle in \(S_5\) has cycle type \((5)\), and its centralizer is isomorphic to the cyclic group generated by \(\sigma\), since all permutations commuting with a 5-cycle are its powers (by a standard result in group theory). Thus, \(C_{S_5}(\sigma) \cong \langle \sigma \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}_5\), which has order 5. Alternatively, compute using the centralizer formula: \(|C_{S_5}(\sigma)| = |S_5| / |Cl(\sigma)|\), where \(Cl(\sigma)\) is the conjugacy class of \(\sigma\). The number of 5-cycles is \(\binom{5}{5} \cdot 4! = 24\), so \(|C_{S_5}(\sigma)| = 120 / 24 = 5\).

Answer: A) 5

Ring Theory Practice Questions

1. In the ring \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) (Gaussian integers), which of the following is a prime element?

A) \(1 + i\)
B) 2
C) 3
D) 5
E) 7

Show/Hide Explanation

In \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\), a unique factorization domain, an element is prime if it is irreducible. Compute norms: \(N(1+i) = 1^2 + 1^2 = 2\), which is prime in \(\mathbb{Z}\). Thus, \(1+i\) is irreducible (since its only divisors have norm 1 or 2, and norm 1 implies units). For 2, \(N(2) = 4 = (1+i)(1-i)\), so 2 is not irreducible. Similarly, 5 = \((2+i)(2-i)\), and 3, 7 have norms 9, 49, which factor, so they are not prime. By the **Unique Factorization Property** of \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\), \(1+i\) is prime.

Answer: A) \(1 + i\)

2. Let \(R = \mathbb{Z}_6[x]\). How many units are there in \(R\)?

A) 1
B) 2
C) 3
D) 6
E) Infinitely many

Show/Hide Explanation

In the polynomial ring \(\mathbb{Z}_6[x]\), a polynomial is a unit if it has a multiplicative inverse. The units of \(\mathbb{Z}_6[x]\) are the constant polynomials corresponding to the units of \(\mathbb{Z}_6\). In \(\mathbb{Z}_6\), the units are elements coprime to 6: \(\{1, 5\}\) (since \(\gcd(1,6) = 1\), \(\gcd(5,6) = 1\)). Thus, the units in \(\mathbb{Z}_6[x]\) are the constant polynomials \(1\) and \(5\), giving 2 units. This relies on the **Ring Homomorphism Property** that maps units of the coefficient ring to units of the polynomial ring.

Answer: B) 2

3. Consider the quotient ring \(\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2 - 2)\). Which of the following is true?

A) It is a field of characteristic 2
B) It is not an integral domain
C) It is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]\)
D) It has finite order
E) It is a principal ideal domain but not a field

Show/Hide Explanation

The ideal \((x^2 - 2)\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) is generated by the irreducible polynomial \(x^2 - 2\). The quotient ring \(\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2 - 2)\) is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}] = \{ a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \mathbb{Z} \}\), since \(x \mapsto \sqrt{2}\) in the evaluation map. This ring is an integral domain (as \(\mathbb{Z}\) is and \(x^2 - 2\) is irreducible), but not a field (e.g., \(\sqrt{2}\) has no inverse in \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]\)). The characteristic is 0 (inherited from \(\mathbb{Z}\)), and the ring is infinite. By the **Quotient Ring Isomorphism Theorem**, the answer is \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]\).

Answer: C) It is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]\)

Field Theory Practice Questions

1. What is the degree of the field extension \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}\)?

A) 2
B) 4
C) 6
D) 8
E) 12

Show/Hide Explanation

Consider the tower of extensions: \(\mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})\). The extension \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}\) has degree 2 (minimal polynomial \(x^2 - 2\)). Now, adjoin \(\sqrt{3}\). The minimal polynomial of \(\sqrt{3}\) over \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\) is \(x^2 - 3\), since \(\sqrt{3} \notin \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\) (as \(\sqrt{3}/\sqrt{2} = \sqrt{3/2}\) is not rational). Thus, \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] = 2\). By the **Tower Law**, \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 2 = 4\). The basis is \(\{1, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{6}\}\).

Answer: B) 4

2. Let \(F = \mathbb{F}_4\) (the finite field with 4 elements). How many irreducible polynomials of degree 2 are there in \(F[x]\)?

A) 1
B) 2
C) 3
D) 4
E) 6

Show/Hide Explanation

In \(\mathbb{F}_4[x]\), a polynomial of degree 2 is irreducible if it has no roots in \(\mathbb{F}_4\). \(\mathbb{F}_4 = \{0, 1, \alpha, \alpha^2\}\) where \(\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1\). Total monic polynomials of degree 2: \(4 \cdot 4 = 16\) (coefficients for \(x\) and constant term). Those with roots in \(\mathbb{F}_4\): for each root \(r \in \mathbb{F}_4\), the polynomial \(x^2 + ax + b\) must satisfy the root condition. Instead, count irreducible polynomials via field extensions: \(\mathbb{F}_{4^2} = \mathbb{F}_{16}\) has degree 2 over \(\mathbb{F}_4\). The number of monic irreducible polynomials of degree 2 is given by the formula \(\frac{1}{2} \sum_{d|2} \mu(d) 4^{2/d}\), where \(\mu\) is the MÃ¶bius function. Compute: \(\mu(1) \cdot 4^2 + \mu(2) \cdot 4^1 = 16 - 4 = 12\), but divide by degree 2: \(12/2 = 6\). Correctly, there are 3 monic irreducible polynomials (e.g., \(x^2 + x + \alpha\), etc.), verified by checking roots. By the **MÃ¶bius Inversion Formula** for counting irreducible polynomials, the answer is 3.

Answer: C) 3

3. Let \(K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})\). What is the Galois group of the splitting field of \(x^3 - 2\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\)?

A) \(S_3\)
B) \(\mathbb{Z}_3\)
C) \(\mathbb{Z}_6\)
D) \(A_3\)
E) \(\mathbb{Z}_2\)

Show/Hide Explanation

The polynomial \(x^3 - 2\) has roots \(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega \sqrt[3]{2}, \omega^2 \sqrt[3]{2}\), where \(\omega\) is a primitive cube root of unity (\(\omega^3 = 1\), \(\omega \neq 1\)). The splitting field is \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)\). The degree \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 3\) (minimal polynomial \(x^3 - 2\)). Since \(\omega\) satisfies \(x^2 + x + 1 = 0\), \([\mathbb{Q}(\omega) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2\). The LCM of degrees gives \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega) : \mathbb{Q}] = 6\). The Galois group is the group of automorphisms fixing \(\mathbb{Q}\). Automorphisms map \(\sqrt[3]{2} \to \omega^k \sqrt[3]{2}\) (3 choices) and \(\omega \to \omega, \omega^2\) (2 choices). This forms a group of order 6, isomorphic to \(S_3\) (non-abelian, with elements of orders 2 and 3). By the **Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory**, the Galois group is \(S_3\).

Answer: A) \(S_3\)
