Question #1

Easy

Which of the following sets with the operation of addition is a group?

(A) The set of positive integers
(B) The set of integers
(C) The set of positive rational numbers
(D) The set of non-negative integers
(E) The set of even positive integers

Solution:

A set with an operation forms a group if it satisfies four properties: closure, associativity, identity, and inverses.

Consider each option:

(A) Positive integers are closed under addition and associative, but there is no identity (0 is not a positive integer) and no inverses (e.g., 1 has no inverse). Not a group.

(B) The integers are closed under addition, associative, have an identity (0), and every integer \( n \) has an inverse (\(-n\)). This is a group.

(C) Positive rational numbers are closed and associative, but there is no identity (0 is not positive) and no inverses. Not a group.

(D) Non-negative integers include 0, so there is an identity, but there are no inverses for non-zero elements (e.g., 1 has no inverse). Not a group.

(E) Even positive integers are closed and associative, but there is no identity (0 is not a positive integer) and no inverses. Not a group.

Answer: (B) The set of integers

Question #2

Medium

In the ring \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) of polynomials with integer coefficients, which of the following is a unit?

(A) \( x \)
(B) \( 2x + 1 \)
(C) \( 1 \)
(D) \( x^2 + 1 \)
(E) \( 2 \)

Solution:

A unit in a ring is an element that has a multiplicative inverse in the ring. In \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\), the units are polynomials \( p(x) \) such that there exists a polynomial \( q(x) \in \mathbb{Z}[x] \) with \( p(x)q(x) = 1 \).

Since the product of two non-constant polynomials has degree at least 1, the units must be constant polynomials. Thus, we need \( p(x)q(x) = 1 \), where \( p(x) \) and \( q(x) \) are constant integers, say \( a \) and \( b \), such that \( ab = 1 \). In \(\mathbb{Z}\), the only integers with multiplicative inverses are \( \pm 1 \).

Checking the options:

(A) \( x \) is not a constant polynomial, so it is not a unit.

(B) \( 2x + 1 \) has degree 1, so it is not a unit.

(C) \( 1 \) is a constant polynomial, and \( 1 \cdot 1 = 1 \), so it is a unit.

(D) \( x^2 + 1 \) has degree 2, so it is not a unit.

(E) \( 2 \) is a constant polynomial, but \( 2 \cdot q(x) = 1 \) has no solution in \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) since \( \frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{Z} \).

Answer: (C) \( 1 \)

Question #3

Medium

Let \( G \) be a group of order 15. Which of the following must be true?

(A) \( G \) is cyclic
(B) \( G \) is abelian but not cyclic
(C) \( G \) has a subgroup of order 6
(D) \( G \) is non-abelian
(E) \( G \) has no elements of order 5

Solution:

By Cauchy's theorem, since 15 = 3 × 5, \( G \) has elements of orders 3 and 5. Consider the possible structures of a group of order 15. The number of Sylow 5-subgroups \( n_5 \) divides 15 and satisfies \( n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \). The divisors of 15 are 1, 3, 5, 15, and only 1 satisfies \( n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \). Thus, there is exactly one Sylow 5-subgroup, which is normal. Similarly, there is one Sylow 3-subgroup, which is normal.

Since \( G \) has normal subgroups of orders 3 and 5, and their intersection is trivial (since 3 and 5 are coprime), \( G \) is isomorphic to the direct product of these subgroups, i.e., \( \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \), which is isomorphic to \( \mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z} \) because 3 and 5 are coprime. Thus, \( G \) is cyclic.

Checking the options:

(A) \( G \) is cyclic, which is true.

(B) If \( G \) is cyclic, it is abelian, but this option claims it is not cyclic, which is false.

(C) By Lagrange's theorem, the order of a subgroup must divide 15 (i.e., 1, 3, 5, 15). There is no subgroup of order 6.

(D) A cyclic group is abelian, so \( G \) is not non-abelian.

(E) Since \( G \) has an element of order 5 (by Cauchy's theorem), this is false.

Answer: (A) \( G \) is cyclic

Question #4

Hard

Consider the field extension \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\). What is the degree of this extension?

(A) 2
(B) 3
(C) 4
(D) 6
(E) 8

Solution:

The field extension \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})\) is the smallest field containing \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(\sqrt{2}\), and \(\sqrt{3}\). First, consider \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\). The minimal polynomial of \(\sqrt{2}\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is \( x^2 - 2 \), so \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2\).

Next, adjoin \(\sqrt{3}\) to form \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})\). We need to find the degree \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})]\). The minimal polynomial of \(\sqrt{3}\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is \( x^2 - 3 \). We check if \( x^2 - 3 \) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\). Suppose \(\sqrt{3} \in \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\), so \(\sqrt{3} = a + b\sqrt{2}\) for \( a, b \in \mathbb{Q} \). Squaring both sides:

\( 3 = a^2 + 2ab\sqrt{2} + 2b^2 \). Since \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational, equate coefficients: \( a^2 + 2b^2 = 3 \) and \( 2ab = 0 \). If \( a = 0 \), then \( 2b^2 = 3 \), so \( b^2 = \frac{3}{2} \), but \( b \in \mathbb{Q} \), so \( b^2 \) cannot be \(\frac{3}{2}\). If \( b = 0 \), then \( a^2 = 3 \), so \( a = \sqrt{3} \), which is not rational. Thus, \(\sqrt{3} \notin \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\), and \( x^2 - 3 \) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\), so \([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] = 2\).

The total degree is:

\([\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 2 = 4\).

Answer: (C) 4

Question #5

Hard

Let \( f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1 \) over \(\mathbb{Q}\). What is the order of the Galois group of \( f(x) \)?

(A) 1
(B) 2
(C) 3
(D) 6
(E) 9

Solution:

The Galois group of a polynomial is the group of automorphisms of its splitting field over \(\mathbb{Q}\). First, determine if \( f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1 \) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Q}\). By the Rational Root Theorem, possible rational roots are \( \pm 1 \). Testing:

\( f(1) = 1^3 - 3 \cdot 1 + 1 = 1 - 3 + 1 = -1 \neq 0 \)

\( f(-1) = (-1)^3 - 3(-1) + 1 = -1 + 3 + 1 = 3 \neq 0 \)

Since there are no rational roots and \( f(x) \) is cubic, it is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Q}\). Thus, the degree of the extension \(\mathbb{Q}(\alpha) : \mathbb{Q}\), where \(\alpha\) is a root, is 3.

Next, find the splitting field. Compute the discriminant of \( f(x) \):

For a cubic \( x^3 + ax^2 + bx + c \), the discriminant is:

\( \Delta = 18abc - 4a^3c + a^2b^2 - 4b^3 - 27c^2 \)

Here, \( a = 0 \), \( b = -3 \), \( c = 1 \):

\( \Delta = 18 \cdot 0 \cdot (-3) \cdot 1 - 4 \cdot 0^3 \cdot 1 + 0^2 \cdot (-3)^2 - 4(-3)^3 - 27 \cdot 1^2 \)

\( = 0 + 0 + 0 - 4(-27) - 27 = 108 - 27 = 81 \)

Since \(\Delta = 81\) is a perfect square, the Galois group is a subgroup of \( A_3 \), the alternating group on 3 letters, which has order 3. The splitting field is obtained by adjoining the roots. Since \( f(x) \) is irreducible, the degree of the splitting field over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is at most 6 (since adjoining one root gives degree 3, and the quadratic factor may add degree 2). However, since \(\sqrt{\Delta} = 9 \in \mathbb{Q}\), the splitting field is \(\mathbb{Q}(\alpha)\), which has degree 3, and the Galois group is cyclic of order 3.

Answer: (C) 3

Question #6

Medium

Let \( M \) be a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-module isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}\). What is the number of elements in \( M \)?

(A) 12
(B) 24
(C) 10
(D) 48
(E) 36

Solution:

A \(\mathbb{Z}\)-module is an abelian group under addition. If \( M \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \), we need to find the number of elements in \( M \).

The order of a direct sum of finite groups is the product of their orders. The group \(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}\) has 4 elements, and \(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}\) has 6 elements.

Thus, the number of elements in \( M \) is:

\( |\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}| \cdot |\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}| = 4 \cdot 6 = 24 \)

Answer: (B) 24
